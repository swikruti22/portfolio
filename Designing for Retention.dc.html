Designing for Retention

Designing engagement and retention for low-motivation users in a therapeutic chatbot.

Role
Lead Conversation Designer · Project Manager
Duration
4 months
Background

According to the National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH), approximately 2.7% of U.S. adolescents aged 13 to 18 experience eating disorders, with a higher prevalence among females (3.8%) compared to males (1.5%). We aimed to evaluate the efficacy of Wysa's eating disorder program (a 4-week program) on a digital platform in supporting individuals dealing with this issue.

Wysa offers structured digital therapy programs delivered through conversational interactions. These programs combine daily check-ins, CBT techniques, and guided reflection over 4–6 weeks.

This project supported the early design and testing of Wysa's digital eating disorder intervention program. Insights from a pilot cohort helped shape the program and contributed to a successful grant application, enabling a larger clinical trial with Washington University School of Medicine to evaluate the intervention at scale.

Problem

Evening check-ins were designed to help users reflect on their day and wind down before sleep. However, engagement was consistently lower compared to morning check-ins.

Users often skipped the evening interaction due to low motivation, mental fatigue, or avoidance of reflection at the end of the day.

The Solution

Based on user feedback and behavioural insights, we introduced flexibility by reducing the frequency of evening check-ins and allowing users to engage once or twice a week if daily conversations felt overwhelming — and we also offered options for winding down. This balanced data collection needs with user emotional capacity.

Outcome

The redesigned evening check-in reduced interaction friction and aligned the conversation with users' evening mental state. The changes supported more flexible engagement and enabled the program to capture qualitative insights from evening reflections.
